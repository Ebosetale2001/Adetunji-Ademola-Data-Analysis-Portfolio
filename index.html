1. Executive Summary

This report presents a comprehensive analysis of Accenture's social media content reaction dataset, spanning 24,573 audience interaction events across 962 unique content assets published between June 2020 and June 2021. The dataset encompasses 16 content categories, four media formats (photo, video, GIF, audio), and 16 distinct reaction types mapped to positive, neutral, and negative sentiment tiers through a weighted scoring system.

The overarching finding is one of strong, consistent audience engagement. More than half of all reactions (56.2%) are positive, and the weighted engagement score totals 973,645 points across the full dataset. The Animals, Science, and Healthy Eating categories anchor performance at the top, collectively delivering over 215,000 score points. The Power BI dashboard distils this into a top-five category view with a combined score of 350,886, providing an executive-ready summary of the highest-value content areas.

Monthly engagement is remarkably stable at approximately 2,000 reactions per month across all full calendar months, indicating consistent publishing cadence and sustained audience interest rather than episodic viral activity. Content formats perform nearly identically on average score, suggesting that category relevance and reaction quality are the primary performance levers — not the medium itself.

Three strategic priorities emerge from this analysis: (1) maintaining investment in the top-five categories where positive sentiment rates exceed 84%; (2) increasing content volume in underperforming but viable categories such as Public Speaking, Veganism, and Tennis; and (3)

optimising GIF deployment, which shows marginally higher negative reaction rates than other formats.

2. Dataset Overview & Structure

2.1 Data Sources

The Accenture Reaction Dataset is an Excel workbook containing two worksheets and an accompanying Power BI file.

  • Reactions (1): The primary analytical sheet, containing 24,573 rows

of timestamped audience reaction records. Each row captures a unique

reaction event tied to a Content ID, and includes the Reaction Type,

Datetime, Content Type (format), Category, Sentiment, and Score

fields. A derived column ('Sum of each Category') aggregates the

total score for each content category using a SUMIF formula.

  • Sheet2: A summary pivot table listing the top five categories by

cumulative score alongside a Grand Total of 350,886 — the source

data for the Power BI dashboard's primary visualisation.

  • Accenture Reaction Analysis Dashboard: The Power BI dashboard file,

providing interactive visual exploration of the dataset with a focus

on category-level score performance.

2.3 Scoring Framework

The dataset employs a fixed weighted scoring system that assigns a numeric engagement value to each of the 16 reaction types. This transforms qualitative emotional responses into quantitative performance signals, enabling category and format comparisons on a common scale. The score range spans from 0 (disgust) to 75 (super love):

The scoring architecture has significant analytical implications.

Because positive reactions carry scores 4–15x higher than negative ones,

the total score metric is not a simple popularity index — it is a quality-adjusted engagement metric. A piece of content with 1,000 'super love' reactions (75,000 points) vastly outperforms one with 1,000 'like' reactions (50,000 points), even though both received the same volume of reactions.

9. Strategic Recommendations

Based on the analysis above, six targeted recommendations are presented below, ordered by estimated impact on total engagement score:

Recommendation 1: Maintain and Deepen Investment in Top-5 Categories

Animals, Science, Healthy Eating, Technology, and Food consistently deliver the highest cumulative scores and the highest positive sentiment rates (84–86%). Content investment in these categories should be maintained or increased. In particular, Healthy Eating and Technology show the highest average scores per reaction (40.4 and 40.5,

respectively), suggesting the strongest audience-content fit and the greatest marginal return on additional content.

Estimated impact: +15,000–25,000 score points per quarter for each 10% increase in content volume in these categories.

Recommendation 2: Increase Content Volume in Underperforming Categories

Public Speaking, Veganism, Tennis, and Dogs show sentiment quality comparable to top performers (83–85% positive) but generate 35–43% fewer reactions. The root cause is content volume or reach, not content quality. A targeted 25–30% increase in publishing frequency for these four categories — without any change in content approach — could close the gap by an estimated 10,000–15,000 score points per category per year.

Estimated impact: +40,000–60,000 combined score points annually across the four categories.

Recommendation 3: Optimise GIF Format Deployment

GIF content produces the lowest average score (39.2) and the highest negative reaction rate (31.6%) of all formats. While the differences are modest, they are consistent. A strategic review should examine which categories GIFs are being used in and whether they are aligned with audience expectations. Redirecting GIF publishing toward Animals and Science — where emotional resonance is highest — and away from more serious categories (Education, Public Speaking) could improve GIF performance metrics.

Estimated impact: +0.3–0.5 average score points per GIF reaction,

translating to ~1,800–3,000 additional score points over the full GIF reaction pool.

Recommendation 4: Target High-Tier Positive Reactions in Content Design

The top six reaction types (super love through heart, scores 60–75)

generate 65.2% of the total score from just 25.5% of reactions. Content briefs, creative reviews, and A/B testing should explicitly optimise for the conditions that drive these reactions: emotional surprise,

aspiration, delight, and strong personal relevance. Formats and topics with a track record of generating adore and super love reactions should be tracked and replicated.

Estimated impact: A 2% shift from positive (average 59.7) to high-tier positive (average 71.4) reactions would add approximately 2,300 score points per month.

Recommendation 5: Address the February Engagement Dip

February 2021 recorded the lowest full-month engagement of the dataset period (1,914 reactions, -4.3% vs. mean). This seasonal pattern should be monitored in future years. A pre-scheduled content push in late January — featuring high-engagement categories (Animals, Science,

Healthy Eating) and high-performing formats (photo, audio) — could sustain momentum through the traditionally slower early-Q1 period.

Estimated impact: Restoring February to the monthly mean (2,000+ reactions) would add ~3,400 score points to the annual total.

Recommendation 6: Separate Dashboard Reporting from Full Dataset Analytics

The current Power BI dashboard surfaces only the top-5 category view (Grand Total: 350,886). For full programme visibility, a secondary dashboard view or supplementary report should present the complete 16-category breakdown. This is particularly important for monitoring the middle tier (Culture, Travel, Cooking, Soccer, Education) and identifying emerging category growth that would not appear in the top-5 view.

10. Conclusion

The Accenture Social Media Reaction Dataset reveals a content programme with strong, well-established performance fundamentals. Engagement is consistent, sentiment is predominantly positive, and the top-performing categories demonstrate high audience-content fit with positive rates consistently above 84%. The 16-type reaction scoring system provides granular emotional intelligence that goes far beyond binary like/dislike metrics, enabling nuanced analysis of engagement quality.

The total dataset score of 973,645 points — driven by 24,573 reactions over 12 months — represents a baseline of approximately 81,000 score points per month. With focused execution on the recommendations above,

particularly volume expansion in underperforming categories and creative optimisation for high-tier positive reactions, a 10–15% improvement in monthly score is achievable without increasing the total content asset count.

The Power BI dashboard provides an effective executive view of the top-five category performance, and its Grand Total of 350,886 should be understood as a strategic lens on the highest-value segments rather than the programme total. Full analytical coverage of all 16 categories is essential for operational content planning and resource allocation.

In summary, Accenture's social content programme is performing well and is well-structured for continued growth. The path to higher performance runs through volume discipline, format optimisation, and a relentless focus on driving the emotional reactions that the scoring system rewards most: super love, adore, want, cherish, and love.

7. Strategic Recommendations

Based on the data analysis, the following recommendations are prioritised by potential impact and urgency.

7.1 Critical Priority — Economy Class & Personal Traveller Experience

Economy Class personal travellers represent the single largest dissatisfied segment (10.2% satisfaction across 33,005 passengers).

Targeted interventions should include:

  • Introduce a leisure-focused service tier within Economy with

improved seat comfort, food quality, and entertainment options,

particularly on short and medium-haul routes.

  • Redesign the Economy Plus product to create a more meaningful

differentiation from Economy (currently only 5.8 percentage points

higher in satisfaction). Economy Plus underperforms as a bridging

product.

  • Offer flexible check-in, digital boarding passes, and simplified

luggage tracking for personal travellers who prioritise convenience.

7.2 High Priority — Digital Service Improvements

Two of the lowest-rated services (Wifi and Online Booking) are digital areas where targeted investment yields disproportionate returns:

  • Upgrade in-flight Wifi infrastructure to offer reliable, high-speed

connectivity, ideally with tiered pricing or complimentary basic

access for business travellers.

  • Redesign the online booking platform with a streamlined UX, fewer

steps to purchase, and better mobile responsiveness to improve the

pre-flight experience.

  • Improve the Online Boarding process, which is highly valued by

satisfied passengers (avg: 4.03) but poorly delivered to dissatisfied

ones (avg: 2.66).

7.3 High Priority — First-Time Customer Conversion

With only 24.0% of first-time customers satisfied (vs 47.8% for returning customers), the airline faces a critical customer conversion challenge:

  • Develop a 'First Flight Welcome' programme offering enhanced

onboarding communication, gate assistance, and post-flight feedback

engagement.

  • Implement a loyalty-linked satisfaction tracking system to identify

first-time flyers at risk of churning and proactively address

concerns.

7.4 Moderate Priority — Age-Specific Service Design

The wide satisfaction gap across age groups (16.7% for Under-18 to 57.4% for 46–60 year olds) calls for age-sensitive service adaptations:

  • For younger passengers (Under-18): Improve entertainment options,

family-friendly seating policies, and simplified digital check-in for

parents travelling with children.

  • For older passengers (60+): Prioritise physical comfort (leg room,

seat cushioning), reduce digital friction (offer staff-assisted

check-in), and provide clear, large-font cabin communications.

7.5 Operational Priority — Delay Reduction

  • Invest in predictive scheduling tools and ground operations

coordination to reduce the 43.5% flight delay rate.

  • Implement proactive passenger communication during delays —

real-time updates via app and SMS — to mitigate the satisfaction

impact of unavoidable delays.

  • Target arrival delay specifically (15.1 min average), as this

directly affects onward transport and is strongly associated with

dissatisfaction.

8. Conclusion

This analysis of 129,880 airline passengers reveals a business with deeply bifurcated performance. On one end, Business Class travellers flying for corporate purposes — particularly on ultra-long-haul routes — demonstrate satisfaction rates of 69–72%, reflecting a premium product that largely meets expectations. On the other end, Economy Class personal travellers, younger passengers, and first-time flyers record satisfaction rates of 10–25%, indicating systemic service failures in the standard fare tier.

The airline's strengths — strong in-flight service, baggage handling,

and an engaged returning customer base — provide a foundation to build upon. However, the digital experience (Wifi and online booking),

operational reliability (43.5% of flights delayed), and Economy product quality require urgent, targeted investment to prevent further erosion of customer loyalty and brand reputation.

Closing the satisfaction gap between Business and Economy class, and between business and personal travellers, will be the defining challenge for the airline's next performance cycle. Success in these areas will not only improve average satisfaction scores but also materially reduce customer churn and increase revenue from the currently underserved leisure travel segment.

Target: Raise overall satisfaction from 43.4% to at least 55% within

18 months through focused interventions in Economy Class product

quality, digital services, and on-time operational performance.
